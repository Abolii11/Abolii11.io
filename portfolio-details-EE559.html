EE559 Control and applications of power electronics

The "Control & Applications of Power Electronics" course, offered by Dr. Luis Herrera in Spring '23, was among the electives I pursued. This course had a structure that included biweekly assignments, a midterm exam, quizzes, and a final term project. A substantial portion of our grade hinged on the assignments, which resembled mini-projects. These required us to employ simulation tools like MATLAB Simulink's SimPowerSystems. As the assignments progressed, we delved deep into topics such as real-time simulations, Control Hardware in the Loop, and Power Hardware in the Loop. Eventually, this guidance enabled us to utilize the Typhoon HIL virtual device for our required simulations. The primary focus of the course was on advanced converter topologies and the modelling and control of power converters across various applications. This spanned utilities, renewable energy integration, and microgrids.
The final project was both challenging and engaging. It centred on power electronics applications in areas like PV source integration, energy storage, and electric vehicles, with a shared configuration of (DC/DC + DC/AC) as shown in Fig 2 below. Reflecting on the project, I can confidently state that I mastered the DC System Analysis. This covered deriving both non-linear and linearized state-space average models for the boost converter, designing its controller, and simulating the boost converter's switching model. Additionally, the AC Side analysis and the combined simulation analysis were particularly captivating for me.
Through this course, I gained a solid grasp of state-space modelling, control, power electronics averaging, feedback control, and state feedback for stabilization. I also comprehended integrator control for reference tracking, voltage, and current controller design (cascaded control), and inner/outer loop control for power electronics pertaining to DC/DC converters like the Buck converter, Boost converter, and Buck-Boost converter. The coursework also touched upon AC & DC microgrids, their control strategies, and optimal operation of energy sources.
For prospective students, I must stress the importance of certain prerequisites to truly appreciate the intricacies of this course. Specifically, "EE 567 Power Electronics" and "EE 519 Industrial Control System" are instrumental. I'd advise students to be well-acquainted with these topics for a more enriching course experience. Without a doubt, this course stands out as one of the top electives for those fervent about renewable energy.
