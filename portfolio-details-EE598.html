EE598 Individual Problem

Before enrolling in Dr. Jim P. Zheng’s Energy Storage course, I sought his guidance on undertaking an individual project for the Spring '23 semester. Graciously, he steered me towards the compelling task of "Development of an equivalent circuit model of fuel cells for time-domain applications." This initiative was structured as a meticulous 6-week plan, commencing with an immersive dive into the intricacies of the PEMFC Cell.The foundational principle of the PEMFC rests on the reverse chemical reaction of water electrolysis. In PEMFCs, a seamless conversion of chemical energy to electrical energy transpires, stemming from the reaction between hydrogen and oxygen.
Advancing deeper into the subject, it was paramount to familiarize myself with the diverse equivalent circuit models of PEMFC. These models elucidate the electrical demeanor of a fuel cell through a streamlined circuit diagram, serving as pivotal tools to predict and analyze the system's performance. Based on their characteristics, these models are bifurcated into two main categories: dynamic and passive. My studies encompassed dynamic models proposed by figures like Larminie, Dicks Larminie, Randle, Yu-Yuvarajan, and Choi, as well as passive ones from Garnier and Page, all in a quest to fathom the steady-state dynamics of PEMFCs. Tackling the core problem statement required an in-depth exploration of PEMFC's dynamic modeling. This journey incorporated multifaceted stages such as System Identification, Mathematical Modelling, Parameter Estimation, Model Validation, Model Simulation, and Model Integration. Post navigating through these phases, I meticulously evaluated the time domain model of Randle’s circuit. The crux of the proposed methodology centered around dissecting the finite-length Warburg (FLW) impedance in the frequency domain, followed by executing an inverse Laplace transformation.
This process yielded the corresponding time domain impedance model, the credibility of which was subsequently affirmed by juxtaposing it with the frequency domain impedance extracted from a freshly derived analytical transfer function. This holistic approach not only demystified the nuances of the system's performance but also paved the way for refining and conceptualizing high-efficiency electrochemical devices. In summation, under Dr. Zheng's mentorship, there lies a plethora of projects intrinsically linked to the domain of energy storage. Engaging with them would undeniably be a treasure trove of knowledge and experience for enthusiasts eager to make their mark in this realm.
