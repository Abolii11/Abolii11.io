EE 570 Renewable Distributed Generation & Storage

The "Renewable Distributed Generation & Storage" course offered by Dr. Kevin Burke provided insights into Distributed Energy Resources (DERs), Distributed Generation (DG), Distributed Energy Storage (DS), microgrids, microgrid technologies, and more. Since I wanted to pursue a career in the renewable energy market, the course curriculum aligned well with my academic goals. The course enlightened me about recent technologies, microgrid operations, benefits of distributed energy resource-based microgrids, power flow analysis, islanded mode operation of EPS, technical challenges of the design, acceptance, and the availability of low-cost technologies for installing and using microgrids. The structure of the class included unique homework assignments, in-class exercises, a mid-semester exam, individual presentations, and a final project. The homework and in-class assignments helped me acknowledge various problem statements, tackle discrepancies and issues, and devise solutions using the PowerWorld Simulator, which also helped me become closely acquainted with the software.

Besides the homework, I was particularly interested in the ppt presentation aspect. The presentation had a rubric that required us to present on a patented product for each of the following: Distributed Energy Generation Technology, Distributed Energy Storage Technology, Microgrid Controller Technology, and related standards. The task led to the exploration of patents such as "Utilization of Distributed Generator Inverters as STATCOM." "Plug & Play with Smart Energy Storage Units," "Reduction Automation Controller Permitting Replacement of Components during Operation," and many more.

For our final project, we were encouraged to select the "Design Project: System Planning/Conversion" which involved converting an existing non-islanded grid section to a fully functional Microgrid System with the ability to operate in an islanded mode. Fig 1 represents the solar insolation report. The project deepened our understanding of microgrid capabilities, including load characterization, UB's current energy profile, and the intricacies of microgrid design. This encompassed charging station expansions, DER and DS, solar array sizing, battery and inverter sizing, wind turbine sizing, storage capacities, and underground transmission cable sizing. We also conducted simulations with proposed SLD, one-line diagrams using ETAP as shown in Fig 2, and utilized software like MATLAB, and Homer Pro(Fig3).

Additionally, we delved into energy management technologies, evaluated design costs, and familiarized ourselves with the necessary standards and codes for a secure microgrid operation. I would highly recommend this course to students interested in microgrids and those aspiring to join the utility industry.
